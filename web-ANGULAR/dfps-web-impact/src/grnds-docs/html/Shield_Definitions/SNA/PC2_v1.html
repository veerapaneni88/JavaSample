Strengths and Needs Assessment Definition

PC2. Cognitive Functioning


  1. Primary caretaker exhibits good cognitive functioning OR cognitive functioning does not affect caregiving.
    • Primary caretaker has no cognitive impairment.
    • Primary caretaker may have some cognitive impairment but demonstrates ability to reason and problem-solve AND cognitive impairment does not affect caregiving.
    • Primary caretaker's cognitive status is not expected to decline to the extent that caregiving will be compromised.

  2. Some concerns related to primary caretaker's cognitive functioning.
    • Primary caretaker may have some cognitive impairment that interferes with some necessary caregiving activities.
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates impaired reasoning and is unable to problem-solve to the extent that some necessary caregiving activities are affected.
    • Primary caretaker is currently able to meet caregiving needs BUT cognitive status is expected to decline to the extent that caregiving will be compromised (e.g., caretaker has advancing dementia).

  3. Significant concerns related to caretaker's cognitive functioning.
    • Primary caretaker has profound cognitive impairment that affects his/her ability to reason and make informed decisions.
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates confusion and is unable to problem-solve to the extent that most or all caregiving activities are affected.
