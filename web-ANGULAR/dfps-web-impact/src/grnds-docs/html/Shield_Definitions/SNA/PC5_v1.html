Strengths and Needs Assessment Definition

PC5. Mental Health/Coping Skills


  1. Primary caretaker's mental health problems do not interfere with ability to provide care; he/she adapts and adjusts to the client's chronic or changing needs.
    • Primary caretaker may have a history of mental health problems or a current mental health condition, but it does not interfere with ability to provide care.
    • Primary caretaker has sufficient supportive relationship(s).
    • Primary caretaker is willing to accept the assistance of others.
    • Primary caretaker understands and accepts the client's capabilities.

  2. Primary caretaker's mental health problems occasionally interfere with ability to provide care; he/she has difficulty adapting to the client's chronic or changing needs.
    • Primary caretaker has a mental health condition or concern that is beginning to affect and/or has periodically interfered with his/her ability to provide necessary care.
    • Primary caretaker has insufficient supportive relationship(s).
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates lack of understanding or acceptance of the client's capabilities.
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates episodes of frustration, fatigue, or anger related to the client's changing needs.
    • Primary caretaker expresses periodic fear/anxiety related to his/her ability to manage the client's behavior and/or meet the client's needs throughout the course of the client's condition.
    • Primary caretaker accepts limited assistance.

  3. Primary caretaker's mental health problems interfere with ability to provide care; he/she is unable to adapt to the client's chronic or changing needs.
    • Primary caretaker has a mental health condition or concerns that significantly interfere with the ability to provide necessary care.
    • Primary caretaker has no supportive relationship(s).
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates no understanding or does not accept the client's capabilities.
    • Primary caretaker demonstrates consistent frustration, fatigue, or anger related to the client's changing needs.
    • Primary caretaker expresses chronic fear/anxiety related to his/her ability to manage the client's behavior and/or meet the client's needs throughout the course of the client's condition.
    • Primary caretaker completely denies the need for assistance and maintains that he/she is able to care for the client without help despite evidence to the contrary.
